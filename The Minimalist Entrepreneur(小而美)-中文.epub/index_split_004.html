Atlanta-based web developer Peter Askew loves to get things off the top shelf at the supermarket for people who can’t reach the same heights he can. A six-foot-eight former high school basketball star, Askew sees being helpful as the pillar of his business strategy, but it wasn’t always that way. When the dot-com bubble burst in 2001 and he was laid off from eTour, a websurfing guide he helped build and grow, he had to ask himself, “Is this how I want to live? Is this how I can be of service to the world?”
亚特兰大的一名网页开发人员彼得·阿斯克尤（Peter Askew）喜欢在超市为那些无法达到他同样高度的人从高架子上取东西。这位身高六英尺八英寸的前高中篮球明星认为乐于助人是他商业策略的支柱，但情况并非一直如此。2001 年互联网泡沫破裂时，他被他帮助建立和发展的网络冲浪指南 eTour 解雇，他不得不问自己：“这就是我想要的生活方式吗？这就是我能为世界服务的方式吗？”

He knew he could get another job thanks to his background in marketing analytics, but he was also disillusioned. Money and prestige were not nearly as important to him as independence and freedom. Eventually he wound up with another role in advertising where he was exposed to a wide range of business models as thousands of new web businesses came online, but in the evening and on weekends, he threw himself into his side projects, learning about web development, domain names, and how to monetize web traffic.
他知道凭借他在市场分析方面的背景可以找到另一份工作，但他也感到幻灭。金钱和声望对他来说远不如独立和自由重要。最终，他在广告行业找到了另一份工作，在那里他接触到了各种各样的商业模式，因为成千上万的新网络企业上线，但在晚上和周末，他全身心投入到自己的副业中，学习网页开发、域名以及如何将网络流量货币化。

That’s how he stumbled on an idea that would change his life. What if instead of buying new domain names, which often took months to rank highly in search engine results, he bought expired domain names, which already had some degree of visibility? Other people were parking ads on expired domains or flipping them, but Askew had something else in mind, the seed of an idea that was driven by the questions he had started to ask himself about his work. Rather than trying to make a quick buck, he would build real businesses around domain names, asking himself with each one, “Do I feel inspired? Is there a real business here?,” and, most of all, “Can I be helpful?”
这就是他偶然想到一个改变他生活的想法的方式。与其购买新域名，这通常需要几个月才能在搜索引擎结果中获得较高排名，他何不购买那些已经有一定可见度的过期域名呢？其他人要么在过期域名上投放广告，要么转卖它们，但 Askew 有不同的想法，这个想法的种子源于他开始对自己的工作提出的问题。他不是想快速赚钱，而是围绕域名建立真正的业务，每次都问自己：“我是否感到启发？这里是否有一个真正的业务？”最重要的是，“我能否有所帮助？”

He experimented with randomly created names and ventures that didn’t quite work before he realized that “the domain name always comes first, the business idea comes second.” In 2009, that domain name was duderanch.com. Askew bought it and launched a directory, traveling to more than fifty dude ranches to meet the owners of the destinations he featured on his site. Eventually he partnered with the owner of guestranches.com, and the two built a curated list of destination ranches around the country. The success of that venture, which he worked on for ten years and sold in 2019, gave him the time and financial freedom to buy more domain names and develop other niche businesses. Some succeeded, others didn’t.
他曾尝试过随机创建的名字和不太成功的企业，直到他意识到“域名总是第一位的，商业想法是第二位的。”2009 年，这个域名是 duderanch.com。阿斯克购买了它并启动了一个目录，走访了五十多个牛仔牧场，与他网站上展示的目的地的所有者会面。最终，他与 guestranches.com 的所有者合作，建立了一个全国精选目的地牧场的列表。这个项目的成功，他花了十年时间经营，并于 2019 年出售，给了他时间和财务自由去购买更多的域名并开发其他小众业务。有些成功了，有些则没有。

In 2014, Askew saw that VidaliaOnions.com was up for auction. Until then, he had focused on information-based businesses, but something about the domain name appealed to him. So did the onions, frankly. As a Georgia native, he knew of Vidalias—a sweet, mild variety that some fans eat raw like an apple. The only trouble was that he didn’t know anything about the onion business. Or farming in general, for that matter.
2014 年，阿斯克看到 VidaliaOnions.com 正在拍卖。直到那时，他一直专注于基于信息的业务，但这个域名对他有吸引力。坦白说，洋葱也是如此。作为乔治亚州的本地人，他知道 Vidalia 洋葱——一种甜而温和的品种，一些粉丝甚至像吃苹果一样生吃。唯一的问题是，他对洋葱业务一无所知。或者说，对农业一无所知。

Regardless, he put down a $2,200 bid, confident that someone in the business would come in higher. (If bidding on interesting domain names like this sounds like a neat hobby, you may be a minimalist entrepreneur.) When he won the auction five minutes later, he was pleasantly surprised, but he filed it away for later and went back to work on other projects.
尽管如此，他还是出价 2200 美元，确信业内会有人出更高的价。（如果对像这样有趣的域名进行竞标听起来像是一种不错的爱好，你可能就是一位极简主义企业家。）当他五分钟后赢得拍卖时，他感到惊喜，但他把这件事搁置一旁，继续处理其他项目。

As the days went by, though, he couldn’t stop thinking about Vidalias. “[The domain] kept nudging me,” he writes in his essay “I Sell Onions on the Internet,” and “after a month, I began to understand what it was telling me. That I buy pears from Harry & David every year, and I should mimic that same service for Vidalia onions. Instead of farm-to-door pears, farm-to-door Vidalia onions.” He saw a way he could be helpful to others, and a new minimalist business was born.
然而，随着时间的推移，他无法停止思考 Vidalias。“[这个域名]一直在提醒我，”他在他的文章《我在网上卖洋葱》中写道，“一个月后，我开始明白它在告诉我什么。每年我都从 Harry & David 购买梨子，我应该为 Vidalia 洋葱模仿同样的服务。不是农场到门的梨子，而是农场到门的 Vidalia 洋葱。”他看到了自己可以帮助他人的方式，一个新的极简主义生意诞生了。

Askew didn’t eat Vidalias himself, but he knew that many people did, both from his own experience and from strong search volume for the phrase on Google Trends. But he still had his doubts. “I’m not a farmer,” he worried. “I have no logistics or distribution system setup.”
Askew 自己不吃 Vidalia 洋葱，但他知道很多人吃，无论是从他自己的经验还是从谷歌趋势上对这个词的强烈搜索量中得知。但他仍然有疑虑。“我不是农民，”他担心道。“我没有物流或分销系统的设置。”

He got started anyway. His first step was to reach out to a trade group that put him in touch with Aries Haygood, the owner of an award-winning Vidalia farm in operation for over two decades that had, crucially, a packing shed. He used his own money to put up a new site on VidaliaOnions.com, and then, “[while] the farm concentrated on the Vidalia, I concentrated on customer service, marketing, branding, web development, & logistics,” he recalled. “I didn’t have other projects that were this front-facing, customer wise. And I discovered I immensely enjoyed it.”
他还是开始了。他的第一步是联系一个行业组织，该组织让他与阿里斯·海古德取得了联系，后者是一个获奖的维达利亚农场的主人，该农场已经运营了二十多年，并且关键是有一个包装棚。他用自己的钱在 VidaliaOnions.com 上建立了一个新网站，然后回忆道：“当农场专注于维达利亚时，我专注于客户服务、营销、品牌推广、网站开发和物流。我没有其他项目是这样面向客户的。而且我发现我非常喜欢它。”

Askew and Haygood estimated fifty orders for their first season. They received more than six hundred.
阿斯克和海古德估计他们的第一个季节会有五十个订单。他们收到了超过六百个。

It would be at this point in the life cycle of a VC-funded business that the investors would start getting frisky. “Six hundred orders when we expected fifty?” they’d exclaim. “Time to quintuple your hiring. Come to think of it, what does the international market for Vidalia onions look like? A few million in ad spending, a viral video, and we’ll have Vidalias trending worldwide. We’ll probably need people on the ground in London, Tokyo, and Sydney. Time to raise another round.” And on it goes. Picture a little kid blowing a balloon up for the first time, until . . .
在一个由风险投资支持的企业生命周期中，投资者在这个时候会开始变得活跃起来。“我们预期是五十个订单，结果有六百个？”他们会惊呼。“是时候将招聘人数增加五倍了。想想看，Vidalia 洋葱的国际市场是什么样的？几百万的广告支出，一个病毒视频，我们就能让 Vidalia 洋葱在全球流行。我们可能需要在伦敦、东京和悉尼派驻人员。是时候再进行一轮融资了。”如此循环往复。想象一个小孩第一次吹气球，直到……

Askew himself couldn’t help but consider trying to accelerate the company’s growth, but he stuck to what he had learned over the years and focused instead on profitability. He knew that the previous owners of VidaliaOnions.com had gone belly up trying to sell salad dressings and relishes in addition to the onions themselves. So instead, he slowly built the business in front of him, figuring out how to sell Vidalia onions to the market of potential customers within the range of timely and affordable shipping from that one packing shed.
阿斯丘自己也忍不住考虑加速公司的增长，但他坚持多年来学到的经验，转而专注于盈利。他知道 VidaliaOnions.com 的前任老板因为试图在销售洋葱的同时销售沙拉酱和调味品而破产。因此，他慢慢地在他面前建立起这个业务，研究如何将 Vidalia 洋葱销售给那些在及时且可负担的运输范围内的潜在客户。

He made mistakes, of course. At one point, he lost thousands of dollars on faulty shipping boxes, an error that nearly shuttered the operation. But he also made small process improvements year by year, like implementing an automated shipping system rather than manually entering customer orders and printing UPS labels. A few years in, the business was profitable, growing organically at its own pace, and he was having fun.
当然，他也犯过错误。有一次，他因为使用有缺陷的运输箱而损失了数千美元，这个错误几乎让业务停摆。但他也逐年进行小的流程改进，比如实施自动化的运输系统，而不是手动输入客户订单和打印 UPS 标签。几年后，业务开始盈利，以自己的节奏自然增长，他也乐在其中。

Six years in, Askew’s pet project has become a full-fledged business, which makes his many customers happy and has a positive impact in the local community. He no longer sees it as just another experiment along with all of his other cast-off domain names. VidaliaOnions.com is becoming his mission:
六年后，阿斯克的宠物项目已经发展成一个成熟的企业，这让他的许多客户感到高兴，并对当地社区产生了积极影响。他不再将其视为与其他被抛弃的域名一样的实验。VidaliaOnions.com 正在成为他的使命：

Honestly, my customers would be quite upset if we disappeared. Last season, while I called a gentleman back regarding a phone order, his wife answered. While I introduced myself, she interrupted me mid-sentence and hollered in exaltation to her husband: “THE VIDALIA MAN! THE VIDALIA MAN! PICK UP THE PHONE!”
说实话，如果我们消失了，我的客户会非常不高兴。上个季节，当我回电话给一位先生处理电话订单时，他的妻子接了电话。当我自我介绍时，她打断了我，兴奋地对她的丈夫喊道：“洋葱先生！洋葱先生！接电话！”
At that moment, I realized we were doing something right. Something helpful. Something that was making a positive impact. . . . It’s immensely gratifying. I feel so fortunate to be associated with this industry.
在那一刻，我意识到我们正在做一些正确的事情。一些有帮助的事情。一些正在产生积极影响的事情……这让我感到无比欣慰。我感到非常幸运能够与这个行业有联系。

Maybe it’s the onions, but Askew’s story brings a tear to my eye. There is something profoundly beautiful in a value-oriented mission and a genuine purpose driven by your own lived experience. This is what being a minimalist entrepreneur is all about: making a difference while making a living.
也许是洋葱的缘故，但阿斯克的故事让我眼中含泪。在一个以价值为导向的使命和由自身生活经历驱动的真正目标中，有一种深刻的美。这就是成为极简主义企业家的意义所在：在谋生的同时有所作为。

The Minimalist Entrepreneur
极简主义企业家

Before I started to write this book, I wouldn’t have described myself as a minimalist entrepreneur. I would have said I was a founder committed to a new kind of startup, one that prioritized profitability over growth and positive impact over moving fast and breaking things. Instead of capturing as much value as possible, I was determined to create as much value as possible for our customers and our community.
在我开始写这本书之前，我不会把自己描述为一个极简主义企业家。我会说我是一个致力于新型创业公司的创始人，这种公司优先考虑盈利而非增长，优先考虑积极影响而非快速行动和破坏。我决心为我们的客户和社区创造尽可能多的价值，而不是尽可能多地获取价值。

I’m not the only one. In this book, you’ll meet dozens of entrepreneurs like Peter Askew and others who are taking the same approach to building their companies. In the past years, I’ve exchanged ideas on Twitter or at conferences with all of them, and the more people I talked to, the more I felt we should label this new pathway that uses the advent of software to democratize and normalize business creation for everyone.
我并不是唯一一个。在这本书中，你会遇到像彼得·阿斯克尤这样的企业家，以及其他采取相同方法来建立公司的人。在过去的几年里，我在推特上或会议上与他们交流了想法，和更多的人交谈后，我越发觉得我们应该为这种利用软件的出现来民主化和规范化商业创建的新途径贴上标签。

Minimalist entrepreneurs are all unique, as is every path to success, but I’ve tried my best to coalesce my learnings into a single, repeatable playbook.
极简主义企业家各有特色，每条成功之路也各不相同，但我尽力将我的学习凝聚成一本单一的、可重复的手册。

The steps to becoming a minimalist entrepreneur map to a function of the minimalist business—and, not coincidentally, to the chapters in this book. Each chapter builds on the previous one—just as addition leads to multiplication leads to algebra leads to calculus—until finally, at the end of the book, you’ll be fully equipped to become a minimalist entrepreneur yourself. You can read it all the way through, but you should also feel free to hop around; everyone is at a different stage in their business-building journey.
成为极简主义企业家的步骤对应于极简主义商业的功能——这并非巧合，也对应于本书的章节。每一章都建立在前一章的基础上——就像加法引导到乘法，再到代数，再到微积分——直到最后，在本书的结尾，你将完全具备成为极简主义企业家的能力。你可以从头到尾阅读，但也可以随意跳读；每个人在创业旅程中所处的阶段都不同。

  • PUT PROFITABILITY FIRST
    将盈利放在首位
    Minimalist entrepreneurs create businesses that are profitable at all costs. Many businesses never intend to stick around long enough to be profitable. Instead, the plan is to sell the business before profits become necessary, raising money from investors along the way. Minimalist entrepreneurs aim to be profitable from day one or soon after, because profit is oxygen for businesses. And they do that by selling a product to customers, not by selling their users to advertisers.
    极简主义企业家创建的企业不惜一切代价追求盈利。许多企业从未打算坚持到盈利的那一天。相反，他们的计划是在盈利变得必要之前出售企业，并在此过程中从投资者那里筹集资金。极简主义企业家力求从第一天起或不久之后就实现盈利，因为利润是企业的氧气。他们通过向客户销售产品来实现这一目标，而不是通过将用户出售给广告商。
  • START WITH COMMUNITY
    从社区开始
    Minimalist entrepreneurs build on a foundation of community. They don’t ask “How can I help?” but are instead observant and cultivate authentic relationships. They spend time and effort to learn and to build trust, focusing on the market part of “product-market fit” (a term coined by venture capitalist Marc Andreessen for being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market) before they build anything at all.
    极简主义企业家以社区为基础。他们不问“我能如何帮助？”，而是善于观察并培养真实的关系。他们花费时间和精力去学习和建立信任，专注于“产品市场匹配”中的市场部分（这是风险投资家马克·安德森提出的术语，指的是在一个好的市场中拥有能够满足该市场的产品），在他们开始构建任何东西之前。
  • BUILD AS LITTLE AS POSSIBLE
    尽可能少地构建
    When they do build, minimalist entrepreneurs build only what they need to, automating or outsourcing the rest. Similarly, minimalist businesses do one thing and do it well. They work side by side with their customers to iterate toward a solution, and make sure it’s worth paying for, before they take it to customers outside of their communities.
    当他们开始构建时，极简主义企业家只构建他们需要的部分，其余的则通过自动化或外包来完成。同样，极简主义企业只专注于一件事并做到最好。他们与客户并肩工作，逐步迭代解决方案，并确保在将其推向社区外的客户之前，产品值得付费。
  • SELL TO YOUR FIRST HUNDRED CUSTOMERS
    向你的前一百位客户销售
    Minimalist entrepreneurs don’t spend time convincing people—they spend time educating people. Selling is a discovery process, and minimalist entrepreneurs use sales as an opportunity to talk to potential customers one by one about their products while simultaneously educating themselves about the problem they are trying to solve for them. Selling this way is a long game built on relationships and vulnerability, not a one-day grand opening extravaganza followed by selling to strangers.
    极简主义企业家不花时间说服人们——他们花时间教育人们。销售是一个发现的过程，极简主义企业家利用销售机会与潜在客户一对一地交流他们的产品，同时也在了解他们试图为客户解决的问题。以这种方式销售是一场建立在关系和坦诚基础上的长期游戏，而不是一天的盛大开幕庆典后就开始向陌生人销售。
  • MARKET BY BEING YOU
    通过做自己来营销
    Speaking of vulnerability, minimalist entrepreneurs share their stories, from struggle to success. The best marketing shows the world who you—and your product—really are. Minimalist entrepreneurs understand that people care about other people, and educate, inspire, and entertain whenever and wherever they can. Instead of making headlines, they make fans—who turn themselves into customers over time.
    说到脆弱性，极简主义企业家分享他们的故事，从挣扎到成功。最好的营销展示了你和你的产品的真实面貌。极简主义企业家明白，人们关心的是其他人，并在任何时间、任何地点进行教育、启发和娱乐。他们不追求头条新闻，而是赢得粉丝——这些粉丝随着时间的推移转变为客户。
  • GROW YOURSELF AND YOUR BUSINESS MINDFULLY
    有意识地发展自己和你的事业
    Minimalist entrepreneurs own their businesses, they don’t let their businesses own them. They don’t spend money they don’t have, and they don’t sacrifice profitability for scale. At this point, it becomes a game to lose . . . and minimalist entrepreneurs don’t lose.
    极简主义企业家拥有他们的企业，而不是让企业拥有他们。他们不花费自己没有的钱，也不为规模牺牲盈利。在这一点上，这变成了一场输不起的游戏……而极简主义企业家不会输。
  • BUILD THE HOUSE YOU WANT TO LIVE IN
    建造你想住的房子
    Minimalist entrepreneurs hire other minimalist entrepreneurs. Instead of following the status quo, they build their companies from first principles, alienating almost everyone. The way you do things won’t be for everyone, but it will be really great for a few people, and if you define your values early and often and tell the world who you are, they will find you. Conventional wisdom about how we work, when we work, and where we work is changing fast. Minimalist entrepreneurs understand there are few rules.
    极简主义企业家雇佣其他极简主义企业家。他们不遵循现状，而是从基本原则出发建立公司，几乎与所有人疏远。你的做事方式可能不适合所有人，但对于少数人来说会非常好，如果你早早定义自己的价值观并经常告诉世界你是谁，他们会找到你。关于我们如何工作、何时工作以及在哪里工作的传统智慧正在迅速改变。极简主义企业家明白规则很少。

Even when you’ve successfully built your minimalist business, the journey isn’t over. Spoiler alert: It never is. Minimalist entrepreneurs know that life is about more than just their companies. The true magic of entrepreneurship is that you and your business can improve the quality of life of many people. And it doesn’t have to be millions; “enough” is what you decide it is, not a specific amount.
即使你已经成功建立了你的极简主义企业，旅程也没有结束。剧透：它永远不会结束。极简主义企业家知道生活不仅仅是他们的公司。创业的真正魔力在于你和你的企业可以改善许多人的生活质量。而且不必是数百万；“足够”是你自己决定的，而不是一个具体的数量。

If you’re nodding along, great. If you’re still skeptical, well, that’s okay too. I have 50,000 words and a few hours to convince you. Just keep reading!
如果你在点头，那很好。如果你仍然持怀疑态度，那也没关系。我有五万字和几个小时来说服你。继续阅读吧！

Chase Profitability, Not Unicorns
追求盈利，而不是独角兽

Building a minimalist business is not a get-rich-quick proposition, but it is a get-rich-slowly one if you embrace profitability, not growth, as the key indicator of your company’s success. Profitability means sustainability. Instead of treading water until a lifeboat comes along to save you—which is how many founders think about raising their next round of VC funding—it means building your own boat.
构建一个极简主义的企业并不是一个快速致富的提议，但如果你将盈利而非增长作为公司成功的关键指标，它就是一个慢慢致富的过程。盈利意味着可持续性。与其在等待救生艇来拯救你时勉强维持生计——这就是许多创始人对下一轮风险投资融资的看法——不如自己建造一艘船。

And while I do think the minimalist entrepreneur mindset leads to a near-100-percent success rate, I’m willing to concede that it may only happen over the course of many experiments. That’s why profitability matters. If you’re profitable, you can take unlimited shots on goal, virtually guaranteeing your success as long as you keep learning from your customers. Most people don’t start. Most people who start don’t continue. Most people who continue give up. Many winners are just the last ones standing. Don’t give up.
虽然我确实认为极简主义企业家的心态几乎能带来百分之百的成功率，但我愿意承认，这可能只会在多次实验中实现。这就是为什么盈利很重要。如果你有盈利能力，你可以无限次尝试，几乎保证你的成功，只要你不断从客户那里学习。大多数人没有开始。大多数开始的人没有坚持。大多数坚持的人放弃了。许多赢家只是最后站着的人。不要放弃。

We’re moving past an era of gatekeeping, where bosses and universities and venture capitalists decide who gets to try and who doesn’t. Information about how to start and scale companies is now available to founders around the world, and it’s cheaper too, which means there are fewer and fewer reasons to raise money from venture capitalists. There’s nothing inherently wrong with raising money, and not all unicorns are evil. I raised money for Gumroad (and, as you’ll read later, have again, but in a very different way), and there are companies like Pinterest, Lyft, Slack, and others that raised venture capital, grew quickly, yet stayed focused on their customers. But much of the venture capital model depends on creating unsustainable growth and destroying successful-by-any-other-metric businesses.
我们正在超越一个由老板、大学和风险投资家决定谁能尝试、谁不能尝试的把关时代。关于如何创办和扩展公司的信息现在对全球的创始人都可用，而且成本更低，这意味着从风险投资家那里筹集资金的理由越来越少。筹集资金本身并没有什么错，并不是所有的独角兽公司都是邪恶的。我为 Gumroad 筹集过资金（正如你稍后会读到的，我又筹集了一次，但方式非常不同），还有像 Pinterest、Lyft、Slack 等公司，它们筹集了风险投资，快速成长，但仍专注于客户。但许多风险投资模式依赖于创造不可持续的增长，并摧毁按其他标准成功的企业。

Why does this happen? The venture capital business is a high-risk, high-return investment strategy in which venture funds swap capital for early-stage startup equity, essentially buying a piece of the future value of the companies in which they invest. For the model to work, the rare winners like Uber, Airbnb, and Stripe need to pay for all the losers. Aileen Lee of Cowboy Ventures coined the term “unicorn” to refer to privately held startups valued at more than $1 billion, which are the lifeblood of venture funds. In fairy tales, people can’t help chasing unicorns—they are nearly irresistible, but they’re also rare, elusive, and nearly impossible to catch.
为什么会这样？风险投资业务是一种高风险、高回报的投资策略，风险基金用资本交换早期创业公司的股权，实质上是购买他们所投资公司的未来价值的一部分。为了使这一模式奏效，像 Uber、Airbnb 和 Stripe 这样的稀有赢家需要为所有失败者买单。Cowboy Ventures 的 Aileen Lee 创造了“独角兽”这个词，指的是估值超过 10 亿美元的私人持有初创公司，这些公司是风险基金的命脉。在童话故事中，人们忍不住追逐独角兽——它们几乎无法抗拒，但也很稀有、难以捉摸，几乎不可能被捕获。

Her mythological metaphor couldn’t have been more apt. Almost everyone fails to build billion-dollar businesses, even the founders who raise gobs of venture capital. According to Matt Murphy, managing director and partner of Menlo Ventures, approximately 70 percent of startups fail, which can mean anything from full liquidation to becoming cash flow positive, which, despite being good for the company, is still bad for the VC. Of the 30 percent still standing, he says, some return three to five times the initial investment, which constitutes only modest success in this setting. The whole system is riding on at least 5 percent of VC-backed companies delivering tenfold to one hundredfold returns to balance out losses and make it all worth it. Without them, the VC model simply doesn’t work. That’s because the outsized success of the rare billion-dollar startup compensates for all the money thrown against the wall, like so much spaghetti, on thousands of other ventures.
她的神话隐喻再贴切不过了。几乎所有人都无法建立价值数十亿美元的企业，即使是那些筹集了大量风险投资的创始人。据 Menlo Ventures 的管理董事兼合伙人 Matt Murphy 称，大约 70%的初创公司会失败，这可能意味着从完全清算到实现现金流为正，尽管这对公司有利，但对风险投资来说仍然不利。他说，在剩下的 30%中，有些能带来三到五倍的初始投资回报，这在这种情况下仅仅算是中等成功。整个系统依赖于至少 5%的风险投资支持的公司提供十倍到百倍的回报来平衡损失并使一切值得。没有它们，风险投资模式根本行不通。这是因为那些罕见的十亿美元级初创公司的巨大成功弥补了在其他数千个项目上投入的资金，就像扔在墙上的意大利面一样。

That’s not what minimalist entrepreneurs do. We are laser focused on profitability from day one, in order to get to sustainability soon after, so that we can serve our customers and our communities for as long as we wish.
这不是极简主义企业家所做的。我们从第一天起就专注于盈利，以便在不久之后实现可持续发展，这样我们就可以根据自己的意愿为客户和社区服务。

Don’t Call It a Comeback
别称之为复出

No matter where you work, how you work, or who you work for right now, you can use the principles in this book to rethink the beliefs and practices that may be holding you back. I really do believe that starting a business should be an option for everyone, no matter your background. That’s why this book is full of examples of many great businesses that have been built by passionate individuals around the world, many who have flown under the radar until now. For up-and-coming minimalist entrepreneurs, I hope their stories can serve as examples as new online tools make the process of building, marketing, and selling easier and cheaper for everyone—including solopreneurs and independent creators.
无论你在哪里工作，如何工作，或者你现在为谁工作，你都可以使用本书中的原则来重新思考可能阻碍你的信念和做法。我真的相信创业应该是每个人的选择，无论你的背景如何。这就是为什么本书充满了许多伟大企业的例子，这些企业是由世界各地充满激情的个人建立的，其中许多人直到现在才被人注意到。对于新兴的极简主义企业家，我希望他们的故事可以作为例子，因为新的在线工具使得构建、营销和销售的过程对每个人来说都更简单、更便宜——包括独立创业者和独立创作者。

Where do you start? Take a good hard look at the people, places, and communities you care about. Where are the pain points? What isn’t working, but might with a little elbow grease? These are all opportunities to make things better through minimalist entrepreneurship. It’s ironic to me how often people go around hoping to find a startup idea while simultaneously complaining about all the everyday stuff around them that doesn’t work properly. “Sure, I could solve that for people with a little effort, but the potential market just isn’t big enough to really scale.” That’s the kind of thinking that this book seeks to address.
从哪里开始？仔细看看你关心的人、地方和社区。痛点在哪里？什么地方不太好用，但可能通过一点努力就能改善？这些都是通过极简创业来改善事物的机会。我觉得讽刺的是，人们常常一边希望找到一个创业点子，一边又抱怨周围日常生活中那些不太好用的东西。“当然，我可以通过一点努力为人们解决这个问题，但潜在市场不够大，无法真正扩展。”这正是本书希望解决的思维方式。

You may already be on your business-building journey, but if you’re just getting started, some business models lend themselves more easily to the pathway of minimalist entrepreneurship. These include almost any kind of business-to-consumer or business-to-business enterprise that has fast customer feedback loops and ample opportunities for iteration like software as a service (SaaS), digital and physical products and services, or connecting people for a fee. We’ll talk about all of these later in the book.
你可能已经在创业的旅程中，但如果你刚刚开始，有些商业模式更容易走上极简创业的道路。这些包括几乎任何类型的面向消费者或企业的企业，这些企业具有快速的客户反馈循环和充足的迭代机会，如软件即服务（SaaS）、数字和实体产品和服务，或通过收费连接人们。我们将在本书后面讨论所有这些。

There are also businesses that aren’t necessarily suited for this framework because of the slow pace of customer feedback. For example, any business that requires a heavy investment in research and development or that relies on sales to large, bureaucratized corporations or institutions—like Fortune 100 companies, academia, or hospitals—might not match up as well with the processes and systems I recommend.
也有一些企业不太适合这个框架，因为客户反馈的速度较慢。例如，任何需要大量研发投资或依赖于向大型官僚化公司或机构（如财富 100 强公司、学术界或医院）销售的企业，可能不太适合我推荐的流程和系统。

The good news is that what constitutes a “business” is changing faster than ever before and opening up possibilities to a wider range of innovators. Though this shift was in motion before 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated it and drove increased interest in entrepreneurship from people of all backgrounds. Now more than ever before, we don’t need to move to a place called Silicon Valley, go to a school called Harvard or Stanford, and raise money from the venture capitalists. The internet lets you learn from anywhere, network with anyone, and raise money directly from customers.
好消息是，“企业”的定义比以往任何时候都在更快地变化，并为更广泛的创新者打开了可能性。虽然这种转变在 2020 年之前就已开始，但 COVID-19 大流行加速了这一进程，并激发了来自各行各业的人们对创业的兴趣。现在比以往任何时候都不需要搬到一个叫硅谷的地方，去一个叫哈佛或斯坦福的学校，或从风险投资家那里筹集资金。互联网让你可以从任何地方学习，与任何人建立网络，并直接从客户那里筹集资金。

The world desperately needs the solutions that only entrepreneurs can provide. Everyday problems are all around us, but they are often hidden from the view of the Silicon Valley software engineers and Ivy League overachievers who have been anointed as our entrepreneurial class. We need the help of entrepreneurs from every part of the planet and every stratum of society. It’s down to individual creators and entrepreneurs to set better goals for ourselves and our businesses. After all, problems don’t solve themselves. People do.
世界迫切需要只有企业家才能提供的解决方案。日常问题无处不在，但它们往往被硅谷软件工程师和常春藤盟校的成就者们所忽视，他们被视为我们的企业家阶层。我们需要来自世界各地和社会各阶层的企业家的帮助。最终，还是要靠个人创造者和企业家为我们自己和我们的企业设定更好的目标。毕竟，问题不会自己解决，人们才会。

Creator First, Entrepreneur Second
创造者优先，企业家其次

On paper, it seems simple enough:
理论上，这似乎很简单：

  1. Narrow down who your ideal customer is. Narrow until you can narrow no more.
    缩小你的理想客户范围。缩小到不能再缩小为止。
  2. Define exactly what pain point you are solving for them, and how much they will pay you to solve it.
    明确你要为他们解决的痛点，以及他们愿意为此支付多少费用。
  3. Set a hard deadline and focus fully on building a solution, then charge for it.
    设定一个严格的截止日期，全力以赴地构建解决方案，然后为此收费。
  4. Repeat the process until you’ve found a product that works, then scale a business around it.
    重复这个过程，直到找到一个有效的产品，然后围绕它扩大业务。

In practice, it’s not so simple. There are many complications that pop up, and most people don’t even know where to start. A “business” of any kind is too scary, too amorphous, or too unattainable. Luckily, there’s another way to get started today. Before you become an entrepreneur, become a creator.
实际上，这并不简单。会出现许多复杂情况，大多数人甚至不知道从哪里开始。任何形式的“生意”都太可怕、太模糊或太难以实现。幸运的是，还有另一种方法可以从今天开始。在成为企业家之前，先成为一个创作者。

That could mean being an artist, but it doesn’t have to. Creators make things, charge their audiences for those things, and then use that money to make more things. They use the first dollars they earn as tools to fuel their own creative drive, not the other way around. With time and experience, creators show others how to turn their own creativity into businesses, and the cycle continues. In the end, there isn’t much difference between a business like Gumroad and a creator. It’s just semantics—one or more people using the tool of a business to make something new. Painters need brushes. Writers need pencils. Creators need businesses. It’s key for people to understand that, because it lowers the cognitive barrier to starting a business, and starting is really important. You don’t learn, then start. You start, then learn.
这可能意味着成为一名艺术家，但不一定。创作者创造事物，为这些事物向观众收费，然后用这些钱创造更多的东西。他们将赚到的第一笔钱作为工具来激发自己的创造力，而不是反过来。随着时间和经验的积累，创作者向他人展示如何将自己的创造力转化为商业，循环不断。最终，像 Gumroad 这样的企业和创作者之间没有太大区别。这只是语义问题——一个或多个人使用商业工具来创造新事物。画家需要画笔。作家需要铅笔。创作者需要企业。人们理解这一点很关键，因为这降低了创业的认知障碍，而开始真的很重要。你不是先学习，然后开始。你是先开始，然后学习。

My best friend in middle school, obsessed with the computer game World of Warcraft, started designing fantasy creatures in Photoshop. I was impressed, but I also remember thinking, “I could do that.” So I went through some Photoshop tutorials and got to work. When I started to get the hang of the software, I began submitting logos to online competitions. I didn’t win any, but the process of creating a lot of stuff and putting it out there made me a pretty decent designer, and then led to freelance web design work.
我中学时最好的朋友痴迷于电脑游戏《魔兽世界》，开始在 Photoshop 中设计幻想生物。我对此印象深刻，但我也记得心想：“我也能做到。”于是我学习了一些 Photoshop 教程并开始动手。当我开始掌握这款软件时，我开始向在线比赛提交徽标。虽然没有赢得任何比赛，但创造大量作品并将其展示出来的过程让我成为了一名相当不错的设计师，随后也带来了自由职业的网页设计工作。

Once you’re working on other people’s projects, you can’t help but get ideas of your own, so I began building simple web applications, hiring developers to help with the coding. For example, before Twitter natively supported threads, I created an app called Tweader to see the conversations that happened between people on Twitter. Another app, Ping Me When It’s Up, would text me when a website that had gone down was back up again. (It should be clear by now that I’ve never been very good at naming things.)
一旦你开始参与别人的项目，你就会忍不住产生自己的想法，所以我开始构建简单的网络应用程序，并雇佣开发人员帮助编写代码。例如，在 Twitter 原生支持线程之前，我创建了一个名为 Tweader 的应用程序，用于查看 Twitter 上人们之间的对话。另一个应用程序，Ping Me When It’s Up，会在一个网站恢复上线时给我发短信。（现在应该很明显，我在命名方面从来不是很擅长。）

When the iPhone App Store opened, I learned to code for it by taking a free course on iOS development from Stanford through iTunes University, called CS193P (I still recommend it!). The App Store handled all the financial aspects of selling what I made to customers around the world, which meant I could focus on creating apps. Perfect.
当 iPhone 应用商店开业时，我通过 iTunes University 从斯坦福大学免费学习了一门 iOS 开发课程，名为 CS193P（我仍然推荐它！），从而学会了为其编程。应用商店处理了我向全球客户销售产品的所有财务方面，这意味着我可以专注于创建应用程序。完美。

The first app I built was called Taxi Lah!, which let users call a cab from their phones—before Uber. I put it on the App Store for fellow Singaporeans and made a few thousand dollars. Then I made an app called Color Stream that let designers create and modify color palettes on their phones. I made about $10,000 from that. Each time, I was trying to solve a problem I had. I wanted to design and build a little bit of software to make my life—and the lives of others—a little bit better.
我开发的第一个应用程序叫做 Taxi Lah!，它让用户可以在手机上叫出租车——在 Uber 之前。我把它放在应用商店里给新加坡的同胞们，并赚了几千美元。然后我制作了一个名为 Color Stream 的应用程序，让设计师可以在手机上创建和修改调色板。我从中赚了大约 10,000 美元。每次，我都在尝试解决我遇到的问题。我想设计和构建一些软件，让我的生活——以及他人的生活——变得更好一点。

This experience—shipping real products to real customers—led to my first job at Pinterest, where I designed and built the Pinterest iPhone app. While I was there, I built Gumroad in my spare time to help me sell an icon I designed in Photoshop. When I found a solution that worked, I sold it to other creators, who sold their products to their own community of creators, many of whom eventually became Gumroad customers themselves. Now I’m an honest-to-goodness entrepreneur without ever having really given it much conscious thought along the way.
这种体验——将真实的产品运送给真实的客户——让我获得了在 Pinterest 的第一份工作，在那里我设计并构建了 Pinterest 的 iPhone 应用程序。在那里，我利用业余时间创建了 Gumroad，以帮助我出售我在 Photoshop 中设计的图标。当我找到一个有效的解决方案时，我将其出售给其他创作者，他们将他们的产品出售给他们自己的创作者社区，其中许多人最终也成为了 Gumroad 的客户。现在，我成为了一名真正的企业家，而在这个过程中我几乎没有认真考虑过。

The App Store cleared the marketing and financial obstacles out of my way so that I could fully embrace my creative side and become a creator. That transition led to me becoming a founder. It’s an upward, virtuous cycle. Creation begets more creation. Today, Gumroad does the exact same thing for other would-be creators. It’s basically a glorified Sahil-cloning factory. Isn’t that beautiful? (My mom thinks it is.)
应用商店清除了我面前的营销和财务障碍，使我能够充分发挥我的创造力，成为一名创作者。这一转变使我成为了一名创始人。这是一个向上的良性循环。创造催生更多的创造。今天，Gumroad 为其他潜在的创作者做着完全相同的事情。它基本上是一个美化版的 Sahil 克隆工厂。这难道不美妙吗？（我妈妈觉得很美妙。）

Max Ulichney is an art director and illustrator based in Los Angeles. He’d always imagined he’d spend his days working at a large company to pay the bills in return for a few hours in the evening to do his own work. He spent fifteen years as an art director at the same creative agency, eventually using an iPad app called Procreate to draw and paint in between meetings. One day, a couple of years ago, Max decided to sell some of the digital “brushes” he’d designed for his own use to other Procreate users. A few hundred dollars later, he decided it was worth the effort to continue selling the digital tools as a business. Two years in, Max was making a living as an independent creator. Recently, he quit his job at the agency to work on Maxpacks full-time.
Max Ulichney 是一位居住在洛杉矶的艺术总监和插画家。他一直以为自己会在一家大公司工作来支付账单，晚上抽出几个小时来做自己的工作。他在同一家创意机构担任艺术总监十五年，最终在会议间隙使用一款名为 Procreate 的 iPad 应用程序进行绘画。有一天，几年前，Max 决定将他为自己设计的数字“画笔”出售给其他 Procreate 用户。赚了几百美元后，他决定继续销售这些数字工具作为一项业务是值得的。两年后，Max 作为独立创作者谋生。最近，他辞去了在机构的工作，全职投入到 Maxpacks 的工作中。

There are thousands of “creator first, entrepreneur second” stories like Max’s.
有成千上万像 Max 这样的“先是创作者，后是企业家”的故事。

Adam Wathan and Steve Schoger teach people how to build and design web applications. They believe, as I do, that nearly anyone can become a competent front-end engineer and designer with a little help. In December 2018, after just a few years building their online audiences, they released Refactoring UI, an online course, earning over $800,000 in a single month. Now they get to spend their time doing what they really love: building Tailwind, a free and open-source framework for the rapid creation of custom web designs.
Adam Wathan 和 Steve Schoger 教人们如何构建和设计网络应用程序。他们和我一样，相信几乎任何人都可以在一点帮助下成为一名合格的前端工程师和设计师。2018 年 12 月，在仅仅几年内建立了他们的在线受众后，他们发布了 Refactoring UI 这门在线课程，仅一个月就赚取了超过 80 万美元。现在，他们可以花时间做他们真正热爱的事情：构建 Tailwind，一个用于快速创建自定义网页设计的免费开源框架。

Kristina Garner, mother of two boys, teaches families how to run secular, nature-based homeschooling programs for their kids. What started as a blog in 2015 about her personal passion has become Blossom and Root, a business that employs dozens of people and helps thousands of families every month.
Kristina Garner 是两个男孩的母亲，她教导家庭如何为孩子们开展世俗的、以自然为基础的家庭教育项目。2015 年，她的个人热情开始于一个博客，如今已发展成 Blossom and Root，这是一家雇佣数十人并每月帮助成千上万家庭的企业。

These are just a few of the 28,207 creators who sold something on Gumroad last month. 这些只是上个月在 Gumroad 上销售产品的 28,207 位创作者中的一小部分。今天这个数字听起来很大，但我也曾从一个更小、更不起眼的地方开始，创作总是从接近零的数字开始：从无到有，现在有了数字画笔、在线课程、送到你家门口的 Vidalia 洋葱。

在下一章中，我将向你展示如何开始。

KEY TAKEAWAYS
关键要点
  • You don’t learn, then start. You start, then learn.
    你不是先学习再开始，而是先开始再学习。
  • Minimalist entrepreneurs focus on getting “profitable at all costs” instead of growing at all costs.
    极简主义企业家专注于“以任何代价盈利”，而不是以任何代价增长。
  • A business is a way to solve problems for people you care about—and get paid for it.
    生意是一种为你关心的人解决问题并从中获得报酬的方式。
  • Become a creator first, an entrepreneur second.
    首先成为创作者，其次成为企业家。
